Week 1 · Introduction

Why does ethics matter in IT?

Technology shapes nearly every aspect of society — and with it comes moral responsibility. This lesson introduces the four major ethical frameworks you'll use all semester to analyze real-world IT dilemmas.

Learning goals: By the end of this lesson you'll be able to name and describe the four ethical theories, identify which framework is being applied in a scenario, and explain the strengths and limits of each.

Utilitarianism in IT

Used to evaluate policies, algorithms, and product decisions. Ask: does this produce the greatest benefit for the most people? A company might collect user data to improve service quality — utilitarianism would weigh that benefit against privacy harms.

Strengths
Good for policy decisions
Considers broad societal impact
Practical and measurable
Limits
Can overlook individual rights
Hard to predict all outcomes
May justify harm to minorities

Deontology in IT

Rooted in Kant's categorical imperative: act only on rules you'd want everyone to follow universally. Applied to IT, it means some actions — like lying to users or violating privacy — are simply wrong, regardless of the outcome.

Strengths
Protects individual rights
Provides clear rules
Good for privacy and fairness
Limits
Can be rigid or inflexible
May ignore real consequences
Conflicting duties are hard to resolve

Virtue ethics in IT

Developed by Aristotle, this theory asks what a virtuous professional would do. In IT, virtues like honesty, courage, and responsibility guide decisions — for example, a developer who speaks up about a security flaw even when it's inconvenient.

Strengths
Encourages strong character
Supports professional integrity
Flexible and human-centered
Limits
Vague — hard to apply concretely
Virtues may vary by culture
Less useful for policy rules

Social contract in IT

Thinkers like Rawls and Locke argue morality is grounded in rules we'd agree to fairly. In IT, this applies to digital rights, terms of service, and community standards. Would users agree to this policy if they didn't know whether they'd be harmed by it?

Strengths
Emphasizes fairness and consent
Good for evaluating digital rights
Grounds policies in shared values
Limits
May exclude minority views
Assumes consensus is achievable
Abstract "agreement" is hypothetical
